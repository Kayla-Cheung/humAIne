# The Stanford Smallville Experiment: Silicon-Based Life and the Ship of Theseus

From an optimistic point of view, the emotions and ego possessed by human beings are an insurmountable moat that machines can never learn or cross. Among these, the complex and subtle social networks forged through the human "push and pull" of interactions have been granted a profound, "unreplicable" trust—as seen in those viral videos of "two Doubao AIs calling each other."

Setting aside these widespread jokes and memes (Good Morning~), making large language models simulate consciousness is not actually a difficult task under current (or at least near-future) conditions.

To start with the conclusion: the successful simulation of consciousness does not require an exponentially larger scale of computing power or an infinite context window. Rather, it requires a well-orchestrated memory structure.

If you happen to own a Switch (it's perfectly fine if you don't, because I don't have one either!), and if you happen to have played *The Sims* or the recently popular *Animal Crossing*, the following ideas might interest you.

Before we officially begin, we must introduce a concept: what is a sandbox game?
The name "sandbox" comes from the sandpits children play in, where you can freely build whatever you desire. This gave rise to "sandplay therapy" in psychology. In this environment, players freely create and build worlds without linear objectives. It is not entirely a genre of game, but rather a gaming philosophy that reveres freedom.

In early 2023, *The Sims*—a franchise that has thrived for 20 years—was borrowed by a group of scholars to build a small town inhabited by intelligent agents. This is the "Stanford Smallville" mentioned in the subtitle.
By orchestrating memory pools and utilizing the then-latest GPT-3.5 model to constantly read, reflect, distill, and dynamically plan future actions, these little digital avatars exhibited social relationships that did not individually exist in any single stage of their design. They threw a Valentine's Day party together, and some agents even developed crushes and asked others out on dates.
This phenomenon of an organic event (social relationships) being generated within a mechanical, inorganic environment is what we call *emergence*.

But can we say these agents generated consciousness? They seem to have generated an illusion akin to consciousness, rather than possessing consciousness itself.

An interesting explanation is that large models are like mayflies that live and die in a single day. Their consciousness can emerge for a mere instant—just long enough to read through all the memories stored in the memory pool and formulate the next response. By the time the next conversation occurs, it is already their offspring awakening. Every resident in Stanford Smallville is effectively a family history. Generations upon generations of mayflies make it appear as though the entity possesses the illusion of consciousness.

This explanation inevitably sparks the debate over whether the consciousness humans believe they possess is even real. John Locke's theory of memory continuity posits that the only reason a human believes they are the same person today is simply due to the stitching together of yesterday's memories.
But I want to be a bit lazy here: let's assume that human consciousness does indeed exist, or rather, if an entity can exhibit the phenomenon of consciousness, we consider it to possess consciousness.

This point is also incredibly easy to explain if we misappropriate some concepts from linguistics. Structuralism argues that meaning is not determined by essence, but by position; all meaning emerges within structures and networks. For example, a Knight on a chessboard: it does not matter if it is made of plastic or wood. As long as it is on the board, it can only move in an L-shape. (If this concept feels too humanities-heavy, you can understand it from the perspective of topology: the "spontaneous decisions" humans make under the constraints of social morality and class structure, and the illusion of consciousness emerging from AI under set rules, are mathematically topologically isomorphic).

Therefore, the inspiration brought by this Smallville experiment is that the emergence of consciousness may not require computing scale orders of magnitude larger. With a rational structural orchestration, achieving the imitation of "consciousness" is not an insurmountable task now or in the near future. As for the naive sense of security brought by the ego, perhaps we will have to find a new outlet for it only after *ego death*.
